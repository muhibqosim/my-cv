Paying back the missed fast: the Islamic guiding principles.

introduction

Coming to the end of "Pre-Romadon Series", we shall be discussing another important aspect to know about fasting in Islaam.

As the topic couched, the Islamic ruling which regulates the payment of Romadon missed fast are built on some certain number of principles. Thus, this writing shall discuss the various Islamic principles on making up of Romadoon missed fast - when and how.

paying back the missed fast

This applies to those who are allowed to skip fasting in the month of Romadon as well as, those whose fast becomes nullified due to one reason or the other. Nullifiers and non-nullifiers have been exhaustively dealt with in previous series. Thus, we proceed. The islamic ruling which guides the make up of the missed fast are thus discussed below:

principle one:
He who skips his fast or have it break without having a genuine reason have to make it up

Although, scholars are of two opinions in this regards. Notwithstanding, the strong opinion is that, only the missed days should be paid back only and nothing else. This includes those who intentionally eat or drink or purportedly intend to discontinuance the fast.

principle two:
It is not a mandate to make up the missed days immediately after the Romadon fasting period

That is, he who have his fast break, with or without a legal justification, is not under force to have the make-up immediately after the completion of Romadon. He has the freewill to determine when to have it paid back.

principle three:
Persons under obligation to have the Romadon missed fast paid back are not compelled to do that successively

Somehow related to principle two discussed above, this principle is making it clear that no one is under compulsion to have the missed fast paid back consecutively - without break. There is discretion right. Thus, the clear distinction between the second principle and third principle is that while the former is as to "when" to have them paid back, the later is as to "how" they should be made.

principle four:
Persons who cohabit with their spouse at Romadon day break have one of three options.

This category of persons have to perform one of three rites as atonement for their act, viz:

  • Freeing a slave
  • Feeding of sixty poor persons
  • Fasting for sixty consequetive days.

One do not just pick any of the three aforementioned; the options are to be invoked serially starting from the first option, and if not possible, move to the next stipulated option and then, to the least. The third option is to be done consecutively except with admissible excuses like health challenge.

principle five:
Making up the missed fast of a decease.

Whether it is right or not to fast on behalf of a deceased person, there are three scholarly opinions regarding the permissibility or otherwise. They are:

a. That no fast should be made on behalf of a deceased person, be it the decease missed Romadoon fast or his self-obligatory by way of vow (nadh-r), which he had made before death overpowered him.

b. That it is legal, right and required to fast on behalf of a decease person be it missed Romadoon fast or self-obligatory by way of vow (nadh-r).

c. That only the 'self-obligatory fast' should be made on behalf of the decease and not the missed Romadon fast.

It should be noted that each of these opinions are backed with relevant authorities. However, the author of sohiih fiq-hus-sunnah adopted and stood by the opinion of the second group of scholars who were of the opinion that both the missed fast of Romadon and that of vow should be made on behalf of the deceased.

Meanwhile, there are still some other conditions that influence such a permission, viz:

1. Where it is proved that the decease, when he was alive, lacked the capacity and ability to make up his fast be it Romadon or Self-obligatory fast, his relative and heirs are not mandated to far on behalf of him nor are they compelled to feed sixty poor persons or to have a slave freed.

2. Where it is proved that the decease, when he was alive, lacked the capacity and ability to make up his fast be it Romadon or Self-obligatory fast, his relative and heirs are not mandated to far on behalf of him nor are they compelled to feed sixty poor persons or to have a slave freed.

3. And if he died having an outstanding Self-obligatory fast, it should be made on behalf of him. .
