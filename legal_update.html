Constitutionality or otherwise of Legislating the Use of Hijab in the country

Whether or not legislating the use of hijab in the country is in contravention with section 4(2) of the 1999 constitution (as amended), and whether it is out of Legistaive powers stated in Part 1 of the second schedule of the constitution as argued by the Christian Association of Nigeria (CAN).

Going through article 60, paragraph (a) of the Second Schedule of the Nigerian 1999 constitution, the Legislative is clearly and unambiguously empowered to promote and enforce the observance of the Fundamental Objectives and Directive Principles contained in the Constitution.

Section 14 (1) of the constitution also provides thus: The Federal Republic of Nigeria shall be a State based on the principles of democracy and SOCIAL JUSTICE. (capitalizing mine).

And Social Justice is defined by the San Diego Foundation (SDF) as "equal rights and equitable opportunities for all". This encapsulates the right to adhere to and practice the doctrine of ones belief without infringement of any form.

The use of hijab by the women of Islamic faith is indeed unarguable; it is a divine injunction upon all Muslim women. It is a core doctrine of their belief. It is not in anyway comparable to ifa costume. It is an integral aspect of Islamic faith; it has no specific time it ought to be worn. Just as contended by CAN, the former is a dressing and while the latter is a costume.

In this regard, any attempt to impose on or force any person to act against his or her own religious instructions is, unequivocal, inconsistent with section 38(2) of the constitution. Any rules and regulations, which could cause a Muslim lady to be denied the right to use her head cover, is repugnant to natural Justice and incompatible with the provisions of the Constitution. And as such, such law is barbaric.

By way of conclusion, as clearly provided for in the Constitution, it is the duty of the National Assembly to make laws that ensure Social Justice.
